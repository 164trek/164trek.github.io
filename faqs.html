FAQs

Good to know

General answers for low mountains, jungle treks, high mountains, altitude routes, and future overseas trips with 164 Trek.

1. How fit do I need to be?

It depends on the trek. A short low-mountain hike may need steady basic fitness, while a multi-day jungle or high-mountain trek can mean long hours, steep ground, a loaded pack, heat, cold, and tired legs. We will describe the expected difficulty for each trip. If you are unsure, join a few training hikes first and see how your body responds.

2. How should I train from Singapore?

Consistency matters more than finding a big mountain. Long walks, stairs, Bukit Timah, MacRitchie, loaded-backpack walks, strength work, and steady weekend hikes are useful. Training should gradually match the trek's expected distance, elevation, terrain, and pack weight.

3. What is AMS, and does it apply to every trek?

AMS means acute mountain sickness. It is relevant to altitude treks, not every trek. Low mountains and most jungle treks do not create the same altitude risk. For a high-altitude trip, the itinerary, ascent rate, acclimatisation, symptoms, and turnaround plan will be discussed in advance. Ask a doctor for personal medical advice or before taking altitude medication.

4. What are the challenges of different treks?

Low mountains can still be steep and hot. Jungle treks may be muddy, wet, humid, slippery, and remote. High mountains add cold, wind, exposed terrain, and longer climbs. Altitude treks also bring acclimatisation and AMS concerns. Each trip page will explain the main challenges once the route is available.

5. What gear do I need?

The list changes with the route, weather, altitude, sleeping arrangement, and whether equipment is carried by participants or support crew. You do not always need expensive gear, but footwear, rain protection, layers, pack, and other essentials should fit properly and be tested before departure. A trip-specific checklist will be shared once details are available.

6. What happens if there is injury, bad weather, exhaustion, or altitude trouble?

The plan may change, turn around, or stop. Where a local guide or operator is engaged, their safety judgment comes first. The response will depend on the trek and may include rest, descent, evacuation, or outside assistance. Reaching the summit or finishing the route never matters more than getting everyone back safely.

7. What if I drop out after paying?

Tell us as early as possible. Some costs may already be committed to guides, operators, permits, accommodation, or transport. Any refund will depend on the trip's stated cancellation terms and what can still be recovered. Those terms will be shared before any payment is requested.

8. What if I join solo?

That is completely fine. Many people join because they want to meet others who enjoy hiking and training. We keep the group small and encourage people to meet during training before a bigger trip.

9. Where does my money go?

Costs vary by trip and may include the guide or operator, permits, transport, accommodation, food, camping arrangements, porters or support crew, and other shared logistics. The known costs, assumptions, and any buffer will be shown clearly before payment is requested.

10. What if the group does not reach minimum size?

The minimum group size may vary because guide, permit, and transport arrangements differ by trek. We will state the minimum size and cutoff before asking for payment. If a trip cannot proceed, the applicable cancellation and refund process will follow the terms shared for that trip.

11. Are all trips beginner-friendly?

No. Some trips may suit beginners who train, while others require prior multi-day, technical, remote, or altitude experience. We will describe the expected fitness, experience, terrain, and risk for each trip so people can make an honest decision.

12. Do I need to pay immediately?

Please message us first before sending any payment. Final payment details will be shared clearly before collection.
